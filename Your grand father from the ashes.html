Your grandfather from the ashes

Preface

“It was somewhere here ?” My grandfather called me and said. It was the days, when everybody had began to ignore him. Every few minutes he was forgetting something he had done a few moment ago and rememberes something in distant. How he had called her daughter, my aunt, Samita. When nobody listened to him, he started speaking to himslef, but I doubted though. He talked like my aunt was there sitting beside him. At first it was hard to listen to his hysteria, then it got easier when we started ignoring him.

“ … it was here, you know I was in Nigeria, It was hot sunny day in the mine. We were all working like dog. Mr Jacob was stickler with lunch. It was one to one and half in the afternoon. Nobody can take minute outside that …”

My grandfather had went to many hardships, he had three children. My father’s big brother, my uncle had been died in accident when he was seventeen just six months after he got married. Grandpa allowed my aunt to get remarried and saw to that. But before all this he himself has left grandma in India, when he ventured in Nigeria for a job. It was ’59, India was independent but Nigeria wasn’t. He thought he would get a job for a manager there but all you plan never works out he often said when he was sane. He had to work in coal mine, which was owned by some Britisher. The whites he called, at times he hated them and the next moment he loved them. I suspected, he was always like that, even in his yonger days. Call it sane or insane, but he might not be so different from what I was listening to.

“ … The red shine, it was same like the Kohinoor. I looked around, everybody was working their ass off. I slipped into my pocket and started working again. The sun was up to my head, but all that day I worked as if I got cold, shivering like a baby …”

“Now, where do you saw Kohinoor ?” I asked. He looked puzzled.

That was perfect question to throw him off his delurium. Sometimes, I enjoyed the stories, hell they were more creative than Bollywood movies. Other days, I pitied him, what a fine young man he would have been in his early days.

“I haven’t seen him but read about it” He said with no doubt creeping on his face. “The description matches” He paused and it felt to me he forgot what he was talking about before. I was wrong.

“Mr. Jacob saw me, I was looking miserable, it might have been a fever, he might have thought” Grandpa started again exactly from where he left. And on those ocassion I felt that he was perfectly sane, capable of reasoning, throwing you off the guard. Those moments were rare though.

“ … That he was kind and got me off the mine and sent me home, it was a small room and common kitchen that we called a home. The diamond was still in my pocket when I was walking back to the residences. Adit, If I allowed it might have jumped right out of the pocket but I had grabbed him under my pocket.” Then he grabbed his dhoti and realized there was no pocket, but I nodded so he continued. “I grabbed him tight. I opened the door of my room, I was sharing with a North Indian. It was funny name. Anyway I looked for a safe place to put the diamond. But there was none. Every place I thought seemed like accesible to that North Indian. It was mine, nobody could take it. It was here somewhere … I’d put down here …” His voice was trailing off. It was routine for him, the story would end abruptly without any climax of sort. I doubted though if I let on the story would never end, and won’t have a climax.

But I let him on. “You couldn’t find a place in your room in Nigeria.” I put emphasis on Nigeria. But he stared at me as if I pronounced it wrong.

“No I found it, didn’t I tell you. It remained in my pocket. Always safe with me. That South Indian wasn’t reliable. He was weird man. Didn’t I tell you. The six months after that, it remained in my pocket and then I got off the coal mine and worked at hospital nearby, they needed a handy man. I was exactly that. There was some funny buissness going on the hospital. The doctors and patients looked at me like I was some kind of ghost. Six months passed and I got back to India. The situation in India then, had improved a bit. I got job somewhere, I don’t remember but I remember my boss was a Punjabi. Was it a woman I don’t remember.” He tappered off again and asked “Where was I ?”

“The diamond” I said with excitement even though I knew it was a mere story. Some charcters that he mentioned they were there before, some weren’t.

“Oh It’s somewhere if only I remember where … It was red shining thing.”

“Wasn’t it in your pocket ?” I asked him with slight irritation.

“Why would I do that, it was precious red thing. In my pocket, it could fell anywhere” He frowned. “It’s somewhere I’d put, the red thing”

“Maybe you forgot in Nigeria” I asked and I was prepared for another story.

“There are no diamonds in Nigeria, it was coal mine alright” He said. “And they call me insane” He whispered almost to himself.

This was all. It was the climax. My grandpa was hysterical and that was the climax. But how good storyteller he was, ofcourse until he forgot about what he was talking. He was hell good with that, for a moment I almost believed that there was a diamond. I stood up and began walking away. I knew he would continue as if I hand’t walked away. I felt pity for him.

“Bring me that photo of your grandma” He called me. “That one in which she had wore a that yello dress”

How would I know that is yellow. All the photos of Grandma had no color in them. However, I guessed which one he was talking about. But I didn’t care, the story of him meeting my grandma was getting old. It was mystery to me how he told that story again and again without any mistakes. He loved her dear, and maybe it was her death that had lead him into this hysteria. Somewhere, in my mind, there was always thought peeping out. If he was dead and my grandma was alive it would have been better. Women are tougher than men my mother always said. I didn’t believe her, but my grandma was indeed a strong character.

Grandma raised three of her children when grandpa was at the coal mine in Nigeria. ‘Alright, your grandfather sent money, but it was never enough. We were three, and it was a hell of job for your grandmother to raise us’ My father always told me. He thought he would teach me value of responsibility. But I never understood before the time. Noone does. He was right about the Grandma though. She was tough lady, she even bear loss of my uncle with smile on her face when others couldn’t cope with it.

“Nobody in this house listens to me” Grandpa yelled. I was already in my room against my study table. ‘Alright, Pa! My exams are next week and stories for next time’ I almost wanted to yell but didn’t. I rose from my seat and walked upto the hall. His face was red with anger. If I didn’t get him the photo, I knew what I would get. I have to stay at the dinner alone with my father. A hearing he called.

I brought a chair and climbed up. It was kind of tradition, the elders on right and to the left I found myself with my hands on a ballon ball. That was my favourite. Anyway, I unhiged the photo in which Grandma was standing against a coconut tree, the place where now a new cinema house was getting built. The photo was black and white. But the descriptions Grandpa told were vivid, and I saw the yellow dress alright.

I handed him the photo. What the hell, anyway It wasn’t hard passing an exam. I waited for him to start but what I saw was very unusual. His eyes was turning watery as he was looking at the photo. He always seemed in good spirit even when he was talking about his late wife. On those days, I doubted he remembered Grandma getting sick and leaving the world and him alone. He talked about granma as if she was alive, and we can’t just see her.

Grandpa wiped his eyes and took the photo out of the frame. Behind the paper there was a letter. I had never seen the letter before . It was so unusual of him to keep a secret letter behind Grandma’s photo or hell any photo. I was curious and for a moment wanted to snatch it away from his hand but I didn’t. He didn’t give me a chance. And in next moment it was in my hand.

“Read it, Adi” He said. “I wish I could read”. Oh, I forgot to tell you but he has almost lost his near vision. In pictures, I could recognize face Grandpa had said. I doubted that. Reading was hard for him though, we were both sure about it. There were times, when I liked reading newspaper to him and listening to his comments about politics, sports, the old bollywood actors. Though, now it was all blabbering after the hysteria.

The writing seemed faint on the slight yellow piece of paper. I pulled it closer, so I could read it clearly.

‘Dear, Adit,

If you are reading this, I am probably dead.’

It’s too filmy I thought and a laugh came out.

‘I left your father a will, but this is for you. I may have talked about working at coal mine in Africa, but for a month or so I worked for a comapny that was mining diamonds. I hate to say this loud but I stole one from there and I’ve brought it here. It was shining red in color. It’s for you and your aunt. The other letter I will have for your aunt.

Your grandfather, from the ashes.’

“I didn’t think you had funny bone ?” I whispered to myself.

“What does it say” He said. It said many things Grandpa. That you were delusional way before Grandma died. That this funny letter didn’t mean anything. That you loved your grandson and your daughter very much.

“It’s letter you had sent Grandma from Nigeria” I said. “You are saying it’s alright, you have got a job at the Hospital. Those things.”

“Actually I never sent her the letter” Grandpa sighed. “I kept it for myself. Your grandma didn’t know how to read. So I didn’t sent it”

“Somebody could have read it to her” I said.

“I didn’t send and anyway I got back in six months” Grandpa said. I felt that he was tired of the sudden rush of emotions so, I took the letter and fold it behind the frame. Hinged back the photo to original place and laid him down on his bed. The eyes looked pale and old. For a moment I thought he passed away, but the thought was too filmsy and he didn’t. He just slept calmly until the next hysteria.

When I got back to my bedroom, the words from the letter came back puzzling me. There was no doubt in my mind that it was all delusional but a thought you can call it a doubt, what if it was all true. What if there was infact a red diamond somewhere in the house, although the chances were less as my mother would have found it first as she was stickler for cleaning the house every six months. But what if there was a another letter at her aunts. Then I discarded all thoughts and fixed my eyes at Newotinian differential method.

Chapter 1

When I was staring at that picture another day, I remembered all the stories which my grandfather used to tell, some real and some of them his mythical adventures. The last story, which had my grandfather in tears was I thought the most hysterical one and yet I wanted to know more. One day, even I asked my aunt if she was having any letter that was given my grandfather. She didn’t remember any, but then again I knew how her memory was. It was the same as her late father. Sometime immaculate and on other occasions hysterical. I didn’t pursue more.

Then on one evening when, my father looked in relatively good mood, I had in myself to ask the question.

“Aakash, I will tell you last time” My father felt the rim of the spoon while eating the icecream and said. “You ought to do better, he was the hysterical one”

I anticipated that however I wanted to pursue more as there are hardly any days, when my father answered my question without any visible irritations. Yes, his face was the telling one. “I know, dad. It’s just that it looked like a real one and I felt that I should understand as it was his last one”

My father never was the thinking one, he always spoke his mind. Never too late, always right in your face. This time however, he stared at me and not in any anger ridden eyes, but thoughtful which I had lost hope on. “Really, Aakash do you think we had a diamond in our house ?” He again took a scoop from the icecream can.

“You would have been eating this daily and not on Sundays” He said with a gentle smile.

“What if he had a diamond” I said.

“And he had hidden somewhere” My father finished the sentence and then looked at my mother who had already finished her meal and was the leat interested in these Sunday’s icecream ritual. “Do you think Shivi, you wouldn’t have found?”

He stopped licking the spoon and gave a thoughtful look to Shivi and then laughed hysterically “Ofcourse you sent it your mother, didn’t you Shivi?” He said.

“Stop Nim” My mother said and started smiling. It was the romance that went on between my parents that I didn’t understand. It was there present only at the most inappropriate moments. Even at the funeral of my grandfather, they looked relieved and cheerful when it came to end. I noticed there was something going on between them, I could look directly in their eyes and see they were syncing on. And yet on days of occasion, like their aniversery, it was completly opposite, a cake and then nothing everybody went to their room like nothing new happened.

I was now losing interest in the diamond story.
